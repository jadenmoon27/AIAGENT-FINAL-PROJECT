# AI Agents Final Project

This repository contains the code, experiments, and analysis for our COSC 89.34 AI Agents final project at Dartmouth College. The project studies how learned world models influence coordination in multi-agent systems. More specifically, it asks whether successful coordination depends only on whether each agent has a useful model of the environment, or whether it also depends on whether different agents’ internal models are aligned with each other.

The repository contains two complementary research directions. The first studies **world-model alignment** by training Dreamer-style world models on the MineRL ObtainDiamond dataset and systematically varying how much training data two models share. The second studies **world models as convention detectors** in ad hoc teamwork by testing whether world models trained on multi-agent trajectory data can identify a partner’s behavioral convention and support adaptive coordination.

In the `world_model_alignment` folder, the experiments investigate whether increasing overlap between two agents’ training data reduces disagreement between their learned dynamics models. Two Dreamer-style world models are trained with different overlap levels, and their disagreement is measured by rolling both models forward from the same initial state under identical action sequences. The main finding is that more shared experience substantially reduces predictive disagreement. For example, disagreement drops from 0.280 to 0.142 at horizon 1, from 0.401 to 0.239 at horizon 5, from 0.410 to 0.252 at horizon 10, and from 0.420 to 0.265 at horizon 20 when moving from no shared training data to identical training data. These results suggest that compatibility of internal predictive dynamics may be an important prerequisite for coordination between heterogeneous AI agents. A repair experiment further shows that misalignment can be partially reduced by fine-tuning a model on a small amount of shared calibration data. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

In the `world_models_ad_hoc_teamwork` folder, the experiments study whether world models can implicitly encode partner conventions in ad hoc teamwork. The key idea is that in multi-agent environments, independently trained populations often develop different coordination conventions, and a world model trained on trajectory data may capture these differences. The main contribution is an ego-matched training protocol that isolates partner-convention information from ego-policy noise. Under a naive comparison, partner identification remains at chance level, but under the ego-matched protocol, world models achieve 91.6% partner identification accuracy after 10 steps. This identification is actionable: by detecting the partner’s convention and switching to the corresponding policy, the adaptive agent achieves a statistically significant coordination gain and performs indistinguishably from an oracle policy. The same study also reports that simply planning directly with aligned world models does not reliably improve coordination, suggesting that the main benefit comes from convention identification and policy adaptation rather than shared imagination alone. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Together, these two parts of the repository present a broader picture of how world models matter in multi-agent systems. The MineRL experiments show that alignment between learned dynamics models can be controlled through shared experience and measured through predictive disagreement. The ad hoc teamwork experiments show that world models can also encode socially meaningful structure such as partner conventions and can support adaptive coordination when used for identification. Taken together, the project argues that world models are not just tools for prediction or planning in isolation; they are also representations that shape how agents relate to one another in cooperative settings.

The repository is organized so that each folder corresponds to one experimental thread. The `world_model_alignment` folder contains notebooks for dataset exploration, sequence construction, Dreamer-style world model training, alignment sweeps, rollout disagreement evaluation, coordination-proxy analysis, repair experiments, and final plot generation. The `alex_multiwalker_experiment` folder contains the scripts for collecting trajectories, training policies, training world models, running cross-play and identification experiments, adaptive switching, and figure generation.

This repository is intended to document the experimental pipeline used for the final project submission. Large datasets and heavyweight model artifacts are not necessarily included directly in the repository. The code and outputs in each folder are organized to make the experimental logic and main results clear.
